Project Summary

Employment Status Prediction & Unemployment Risk Profiling

CMPS 262 Capstone | Yeol Ban and Charbel Dawlabani | Spring 2026

Overview

This project built a machine learning system to predict whether a U.S. labor-force participant is employed or unemployed using demographic and household information from the December 2025 Current Population Survey. The main goal was not only to classify employment status, but also to identify which groups face the highest unemployment risk and explain why those risks appear.

Data Used

The team used December 2025 CPS Basic Monthly microdata with about 121,000 original records. After filtering out people under 16, individuals not in the labor force, and armed forces personnel, the final modeling dataset contained 44,544 records. The unemployment rate in this filtered dataset was 3.82%, making the task a highly imbalanced classification problem.

The features used included:

  • Age and age bands
  • Education level
  • Sex
  • Race and Hispanic origin
  • Marital status
  • Census region
  • Citizenship status
  • Family income bracket

Exploratory Analysis

The exploratory data analysis showed several strong patterns. Younger workers, especially ages 16 to 19, had unemployment rates much higher than prime-age workers. Lower education levels were also strongly associated with unemployment. Black and Hispanic workers showed higher unemployment rates than White and Asian workers. The most important interaction observed was that young individuals with low education faced especially elevated unemployment risk.

Modeling Approach

Two supervised learning models were trained: logistic regression and random forest. Logistic regression was selected as the main model because it is easier to interpret, which is important for risk profiling. Random forest was included as a comparison model to capture possible nonlinear relationships. The project used an 80/20 stratified train-test split along with 5-fold stratified cross-validation to evaluate model stability.

Because the dataset was strongly imbalanced, the team also applied calibration and threshold tuning. Isotonic calibration improved probability reliability, and threshold selection was optimized for the unemployed class instead of using the default 0.5 cutoff.

Key Results

  • The primary calibrated logistic regression model achieved an F1 score of 0.19 for the unemployed class.
  • The model reached a ROC-AUC of about 0.72, showing useful ranking ability.
  • Threshold tuning improved the minority-class F1 score by roughly 38% compared with the default threshold.
  • Population-weighted evaluation closely matched unweighted results, suggesting the findings are representative.
  • Logistic regression and random forest performed similarly, indicating the signal is largely linear and additive.

Main Risk Factors

The model identified several major drivers of unemployment risk. The strongest risk-increasing factors were low education, lower family income, Black race, certain regional effects, and some marital-status categories. The strongest protective factors were being in prime working ages, having higher income, and holding higher educational credentials. Overall, family income and age were the most influential features.

Interpretability

SHAP explanations were used to interpret both global patterns and individual predictions. These explanations helped confirm the model's main feature rankings and made it possible to show why a specific person received a high-risk or low-risk unemployment prediction. This makes the system more transparent and easier to audit.

Conclusions

The project demonstrated that unemployment risk can be meaningfully predicted using demographic and household characteristics, even in a highly imbalanced setting. The model is most useful for risk stratification and demographic profiling rather than exact high-precision individual targeting. Findings aligned well with established labor-economics patterns, especially the importance of age, education, and income.

Limitations and Future Work

  • The analysis used only a single month of CPS data, so it does not capture time trends.
  • The feature set did not include industry, occupation, or local labor market information.
  • The model identifies associations rather than causal effects.
  • Future work could extend the study across multiple months, add richer labor-market features, and include fairness analysis.
